<!-- Relevant Facts -->
<details open>
  <summary style="font-size: 1.5em; font-weight: bold; cursor: pointer;">Relevant Facts</summary>
  <div style="margin-left: 0; padding-left: 0; margin-top: 10px;">
    1. The FAA historically had an excellent safety record (DOT, 2020).  
       <em>i. This led to a system of public trust and overconfidence in engineering.</em><br><br>
    2. 94% of certification for aircraft manufacturers was performed by the manufacturers themselves (DOT, 2020).  
       <em>i. This created a conflict of interest and incentivized prioritizing finances over safety due to the lack of independent oversight.</em><br><br>
    3. MCAS relied on only one of the two AoA sensors (DOT, 2020).  
       <em>i. The lack of redundancy made pilots more likely to face a system failure.</em><br><br>
    4. Boeing advertised MCAS as a modification to the prior trim system (DOT, 2020).  
       <em>i. This was driven by a desire to reduce scrutiny, leading the FAA to focus on other technologies.</em><br><br>
    5. The FAA lacked the bandwidth to avoid delegating certification tasks to manufacturers (DOT, 2020).  
       <em>i. Limited government involvement, strained resources, and rising private-sector self-regulation reduced FAA oversight capacity.</em><br><br>
    6. Pilots did not receive adequate instructions on how to respond to MCAS (DOT, 2020).  
       <em>i. Poor communication meant pilots could not properly react to an MCAS malfunction.</em><br><br>
    7. Boeing was actively updating MCAS software when the second crash occurred (DOT, 2020).  
       <em>i. FAA and Boeing stakeholders decided the risk of “live” updates was acceptable, leaving pilots exposed to an unresolved system issue.</em><br><br>
    8. Boeing increased MCAS AoA authority without informing the FAA (DOT, 2020).  
       <em>i. Pilots and the FAA were unaware of the expanded system authority, resulting in insufficient scrutiny.</em><br><br>
    9. Some Boeing engineers discovered that the AoA disagreement alert was only included in an optional bundle (DOT, 2020).  
       <em>i. Engineers did not escalate the issue beyond initial management, preventing senior leadership from being informed.</em><br><br>
    10. Flights before the MAX crashes exhibited MCAS-related issues, but pilots did not communicate them thoroughly (DOT, 2020).  
        <em>i. Pilots on the doomed flights were unaware of earlier issues, and technicians cleared aircraft despite indications of problems.</em><br><br>
    11. During the crash sequence, pilots initially recovered by disabling automatic stabilizer control but then reenabled it (linked to faulty MCAS).  
        <em>i. Inadequate communication about MCAS meant pilots did not understand the consequences of reenabling these systems, turning a recoverable issue into an unrecoverable dive.</em><br><br>
    12. In the years preceding the crashes, Boeing engineers reported significant “undue pressure” (DOT, 2020).  
        <em>i. This pressure discouraged escalation of safety concerns due to low confidence in the reporting process.</em><br><br>
    13. Senior Boeing representative Mark Forkner did not report MCAS’s expanded role in MAX control software.  
        <em>i. Had the FAA known of its increased reliance, the system would have received more rigorous scrutiny.</em><br><br>
    14. Some engineers expressed early concerns about the lack of redundancy in the MCAS design (DOT, 2020).  
        <em>i. Despite these concerns, they did not escalate the issue, resulting in MCAS depending on a single AoA sensor and increasing system-failure likelihood (PBS, 2024).</em>
  </div>
</details>
